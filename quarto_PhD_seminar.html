The Standard Model

The most successful theory of fundamental interactions (so far)

Gauge principle:
Interactions arise by requiring invariance under local symmetry transformations.

  • Electromagnetic → \(\mathrm{U}(1)_{\text{em}}\)
    acts on charged particles
  • Weak → \(\mathrm{SU}(2)_L\)
    responsable of radioactive decays
  • Strong → \(\mathrm{SU}(3)_C\)
    binds quarks inside hadrons

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{SM}} \;=\; \mathcal{L}_{\text{gauge}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{matter}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{Higgs}}\]

Gravity unfortunately is not included in the Standard Model.

QCD in the Continuum

\[\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{QCD}} =\textcolor{#EB811B}{-\tfrac{1}{2}\mathrm{Tr}\left[F_{\mu\nu} F^{\mu\nu}\right]} +\sum_{f=1}^{N_f} \bar{\psi}_f \big( i \gamma^\mu D_\mu - m_f \big)\psi_f\]

  • Gauge fields:

    \[\normalsize A_\mu(x) = \sum_{a=1}^8 A_\mu^a(x)\, T^a\]

    • \(\small \left[ T_a,T_b\right]= i f_{abc}T_c\) and \(\small \left\{ T_a,T_b\right\}= \frac 1 2 \delta_{ab}\)
    • \(\small A_\mu(x)\) encapsulates the 8 gluons
    • \(\small A_\mu^a(x)\in \mathfrak{su}(3)\).
  • Field strength tensor:

\[ F_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu - i g \,[A_\mu, A_\nu] \]

  • Gauge transformations:
    \(\small \forall\; U(x) \in \mathrm{SU}(3)\)

    \[ \begin{array}{rcl} A_\mu & \longrightarrow & UA_\mu U^\dagger \\ F_{\mu\nu} & \longrightarrow & U\,F_{\mu\nu}\,U^\dagger \end{array} \]

  • Covariance:
    • \(\small F_{\mu\nu}\) transforms homogeneously
    • \(\small \mathrm{Tr}[F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu}]\) is gauge invariant

Pure gauge → already highly non-trivial:
confinement, glueballs, non-linear self-interactions…

QCD in the Continuum

\[\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{QCD}} = -\tfrac{1}{2}\mathrm{Tr}\left[F_{\mu\nu} F^{\mu\nu}\right] +\textcolor{#EB811B}{\sum_{f=1}^{N_f} \bar{\psi}_f \big( i \gamma^\mu D_\mu - m_f \big)\psi_f}\]

  • Quark fields:
    • \(\small \psi_f(x)\) → flavor \(\small f=1,\dots,N_f\)
    • Real world → \(\small N_f = 6\)
    • Fundamental rep. of \(\small \mathrm{SU}(3)_C\)
      • \(\small \psi_f(x)\) → \(\small (\textcolor{red}{\psi_f(x)}, \textcolor{green}{\psi_f(x)}, \textcolor{blue}{\psi_f(x)})\)
  • Covariant derivative:
    \[D_\mu = \partial_\mu - i g A_\mu\]
    • Couples quarks to gluons
    • Ensures local gauge invariance
  • Gauge transformations:
    \(\small \forall\; U(x) \in \mathrm{SU}(3)\)

    \[ \begin{array}{rcl} \psi_f & \longrightarrow & U\,\psi_f \\ \bar{\psi}_f & \longrightarrow & \bar{\psi}_f\,U^\dagger \\ D_\mu \psi_f & \longrightarrow & U\,D_\mu \psi_f \end{array} \]

  • Facts:
    Quarks are never observed as free particles
    • Combined into color singlets → hadrons
      • mesons ( \(\small \pi^{\pm}, \pi^0\dots\))
      • baryons ( \(\small p,n\dots\))

Interactions in QCD

Gauge invariance not only dictates the kinetic terms, but also fixes the interaction structure:

\(\small \sim g \bar{\psi}\psi A\)

\(\small \sim g \partial A A A \quad \quad \quad \sim g^2 AAAA\)

  • QED-like vertex
    • quark–gluon interaction
    • analogous to electron–photon in QED
  • New in QCD
    • 3-gluon and 4-gluon self-interactions
    • consequence of non-abelian \(\mathrm{SU}(3)_C\)
    • lead to confinement, asymptotic freedom, glueballs…

Quantum corrections lead to divergencies:
Interactions are treated in the renormalization framework → running of the coupling \(g(\mu)\).

Running of the Coupling

  • Scale dependent coupling: \(\small g \,\longrightarrow\, g(\mu)\)
  • Governed by the β-function:
    \[\small \mu \frac{dg}{d\mu} = \beta(g), \quad \beta(g) = -\beta_0 \frac{g^3}{16\pi^2} + \mathcal{O}(g^5) \] with \(\small \;\beta_0 = 11 - \tfrac{2}{3} N_f\)
  • Solution at 1-loop:
    \[\small \alpha_s(\mu) \equiv \frac{g^2(\mu)}{4\pi} = \frac{1}{\beta_0 \ln(\mu^2 / \Lambda_{\text{QCD}}^2)} \]

  • Consequences:
    • \(\small \mu \to \infty \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; \alpha_s(\mu) \to 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \color{#EB811B}\text{asymptotic freedom}\)
    • \(\small \mu \to \Lambda_{\text{QCD}} \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; \alpha_s(\mu) \to \infty \;\Rightarrow\; \color{#EB811B}\text{confinement}\)

QCD becomes strongly coupled in the IR → non-perturbative tools are required!

Confinement

  • At low energies \(\small \alpha_s(\mu) \gg 1\)
  • QCD potential:

    \[\small V(r) = -\,\frac{4}{3}\frac{\alpha_s}{r} + \sigma r \quad \quad \quad \;\sigma \approx 0.9\ \mathrm{GeV/fm} \]

  • Force:

    \[ \small |dV/dr|_{\mathrm{QCD}} \approx 0.9 \times 1.6\cdot10^{-10} \text{J}/10^{-15}\ \text{m} \approx 1.6\cdot10^{4}\ \text{N} \]

\(\small |dV/dr|_{\mathrm{QED}} \approx 2.3 \cdot 10^{2} \ \mathrm{N}\)

QCD at Low Energies

  • For \(\mu \sim \Lambda_{\text{QCD}}\):
    • \(\alpha_s(\mu)\) is large
    • Perturbation theory breaks down
  • QCD physics is dominated by non-perturbative phenomena:
    • Confinement
    • Mass gap
    • Hadron spectrum & structure
  • Need appropriate Low Enery tools:
    • Lattice QCD (first-principles numerical approach)
    • Effective Field Theories (low-energy approximations)

Lattice QCD

  • Euclidean formulation:
    Discretize space-time as a 4D grid
  • Introduces two natural cutoffs:
    • UV : lattice spacing \(a\)
    • IR : finite size \(L\)
  • Fields on the lattice:
    • Quarks: \(\;\psi(x) \;\;\to\;\; a^{-3/2}\psi(n)\) (sites)
    • Gauge fields: \(\;A_\mu(x) \;\;\to\;\; U_\mu(n) \in \mathrm{SU}(3)\) (links)
  • Gauge links act as parallel transporters
  • Boundary conditions:
    • Periodic (PBC) for gauge fields
    • Anti-periodic (APBC) for fermions

QCD becomes a set of dimensionless variables
It can be solved numerically using Monte Carlo techniques

Monte Carlo methods

\[ \langle \mathcal{O}\rangle = \frac{1}{Z}\int [dU]\textcolor{blue}{[d\bar\psi d\psi]}\,\mathcal{O}\, e^{-S_G[U]-\textcolor{blue}{\bar\psi} D[U]\textcolor{blue}{\psi}} = \frac{1}{Z}\int [dU]\,\mathcal{O}_F[U]\,\textcolor{blue}{\det D[U]}\,e^{-S_G[U]} \]

  1. Importance sampling Draw gauge configurations \(\small \left\{ U_\mu^{(i)} \right\}\) from:

    \[ \mathcal{P}[U]\propto e^{-S_G[U]}\,\det D[U] \]

  1. \(\small \forall i\) compute observable \(\langle\mathcal{O}\rangle_F[U^{(i)}]\)
  1. Ensemble average (estimator)

    \[ \overline{\mathcal{O}} \;=\; \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \langle\mathcal{O}\rangle_F[U^{(i)}] \;=\; \langle\mathcal{O}\rangle \;+\; \delta\mathcal{O} \]

  1. Set the physical scale \(\small a\) (e.g. hadron masses)
  1. Continuum / infinite-volume limits:
  • Remove the UV (\(\small a \to 0\)) and IR (\(\small L \to \infty\)) cut-off
  • (if needed) chiral extrapolation \(\small m_q\to m_q^{\rm phys}\)

Why we need strong machines?

\[ \text{Computational cost} \sim c \;\times\; (\# \text{confs}) \;\times\; \left(\frac{1}{M_\pi}\right)^6 \;\times\; L^5 \;\times\; (a^{-1})^7 \]

  • Cost grows rapidly when approaching:
    • physical pion masses
    • large volumes
    • fine lattice spacing
  • At the physical point with \(L=32\):
    • Generating \(3\times 10^3\)
      → \(2\times 10^6\) core hours configurations
    • Without parallelization on a stand-alone PC
      → ~230 years
  • Data per configuration: \(\mathcal{O}(1\text{-}50)\) GB
    → Tera/Petabytes of data generated

HPC resources provide:

  1. Hundreds of nodes with CPUs/GPUs

  2. High memory per node

  3. High network bandwidth

A remark:

“Numerical simulations have the reputation of being an approximate method that mainly serves to obtain qualitative information on the behaviour of complex systems.

This is, however, not so in Lattice QCD, where the simulations produce results that are exact (on the given lattice) up to statistical errors. The systematic uncertainties related to a non-zero lattice spacing and finite volume, still need to be investigated, but these effects are theoretically well understood, and can usually be brought under control.”

— M. Lüscher
“Lattice QCD - From Quarks Confinement to Asymptotic Freedom”

Chiral Symmetry in QCD

  • Massless QCD Lagrangian has
    \(\small \mathrm{SU}(N_f)_L \times \mathrm{SU}(N_f)_R\) symmetry
  • Exact symmetry? No!
    → We do not observe parity doubling
  • Chiral symmetry is broken:
    • Spontaneously: \(\langle \bar{\psi}\psi \rangle \neq 0\)
    • Explicitly: quark masses \(m_q \neq 0\)
  • Consequence: Pseudo–Goldstone bosons
    → light pions emerge
  • Gell-Mann–Oakes–Renner (GMOR) relation:

    \[\small M_\pi^2 \;=\; (m_u + m_d)\frac{\langle \bar{\psi}\psi\rangle}{f_\pi^2} \]

Why an EFT?
  • At low energies, quarks & gluons are not convenient d.o.f.
  • Relevant excitations are pions
How?
  • Keep underlying symmetries
  • Expand in \(p/\Lambda\) and \(m_q\)
  • Emergence of Low Energy Constants

(A few) Open Problems and Challenges

Topological Freezing

  • No trivial topology
  • Special classical solutions of QCD
    → Istantons
  • Carry an integer topological charge \(Q\)
  • Transitions between sectors
    → tunneling over energy barriers

Twofold Problem:

  1. Simulation issues:
    → Std Alghorithms fail as \(\small a \to 0\)
  2. Phenomenological issues:
    → Thermodynamics of the early universe
    → Cold and Hot Axion Phenomenology
    → Singlet particle properties

Possible Solutions

  • Parallel Tempering
    • Run several copies at different temperatures or boundary conditions simultaneously
    • Swap configurations between them:
      • 1 “physical”
      • N “eased” replicas
  • Works well in pure gauge
  • Fermionic determinant?
  • Requires fine-tuning of the tower of copies
  • Other possible solutions:
    • Metadynamics
    • Open Boundary conditions
    • Normalizing Flows?

Inverse Problem/ Analytical Continuation

Correlators are related to spectral function via an inverse Fourier transform

\[\small C(t)=\int dE\; \exp{\left[-Et\right]} \rho(E) \]

in Minkowski time

  • Simulations are performed in Euclidean time
  • It means solving an inverse Laplace problem
  • This is ill-posed and numerically unstable

\[\begin{aligned}\small \hat \rho_\sigma(E_0) \;=\; \int dE \; \sigma(E,E_0)\,\rho(E)\\ \small C_\sigma(t;E_0) \;=\; \int dE \; e^{-Et} \,\rho_\sigma(E). \end{aligned} \]

  • Backus-Gilbert method
    • Construct smeared spectral function
  • Bayesian approaches (MEM, BR, etc.)
  • Other approaches:
    • Neural networks, Deep-learning…

Application to the Sphaleron Rate

  • Sphaleron rate ⇔ frequency of topological transitions
  • Measure topological charge correlator in Euclidean time
    \[\small C(t) = \langle Q(t) Q(0) \rangle \]
  • Need real-time sphaleron rate:
    \[\small \Gamma_\mathrm{sphal} \sim \lim_{\omega\to 0} \frac{\rho(\omega)}{\omega} \]
  • Challenges: ill-posed, noisy data, limited temporal extent
  • Application of Backus-Gilbert method

\[ \small \frac{\Gamma_\mathrm{sphal}}{T^4} = 0.079(25) \qquad T=1.24T_c \]
